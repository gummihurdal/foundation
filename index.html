Solidifying a Relationship

The proven method — ten practices, and the evidence for each

Note on this document This is the method that Bond runs on — set out in full, with its proof. Each practice states what to do, then the finding it rests on, then the source. The evidence is real but uneven: some points rest on decades of replicated work, others on a single line of research or on clinical theory. Where a claim is weaker, it is marked as such rather than dressed up. Read the limitations before treating any of this as settled.

The practices

Ten
01

Stay warm in conflict

No coldness, no silence as punishment. We ask directly and kindly.

In practice

  • Say the complaint, not the character: "the kitchen got left" — not "you're lazy"
  • If you go quiet, name it: "I need twenty minutes, then I'm back"
  • Never let silence do the arguing for you

Evidence

Of the four behaviours Gottman identifies as corrosive — criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling — contempt is the single strongest predictor of dissolution, and stonewalling (emotional withdrawal) compounds it. Physiological flooding during conflict predicts later separation. Gottman & Levenson (1992, 2000)1
Strong effect · prediction rates disputed
02

Repair the same day

No unresolved hurt goes to sleep with us.

In practice

  • Repair does not require agreement — only contact: "I hate this. I'm still here."
  • A clumsy repair accepted beats an elegant one delivered late
  • The one who notices first goes first. Not the one who was wronged

Evidence

Conflict frequency does not separate stable couples from unstable ones. What separates them is whether repair attempts are made — and, critically, whether they are received. Masters of relationships fight; they just recover. Gottman & Silver (1999); Gottman & Levenson (1992)1,2
Well replicated
03

Raise things at 20%, not 90%

Small and early, spoken or written. No suffering alone overnight.

In practice

  • The rule of thumb: if you noticed it twice, say it once
  • Written counts — a message at 20% is worth more than a conversation at 90%
  • Early raising feels petty. Do it anyway; that's the whole point

Evidence

Direct, engaged communication feels worse in the moment but predicts better problem resolution over time, while avoidance predicts the reverse. Overall and McNulty's key qualification: the "best" communication depends on the severity of the problem — soft approaches work for small issues, and can fail on serious ones. Overall & McNulty (2017)3
Well supported · context-dependent
04

The past is closed

Fully accepted, history included. It never enters a disagreement.

In practice

  • No history as ammunition — a past fact raised in a present fight is a weapon, not a topic
  • If the urge to ask for details arrives, notice it and let it pass unfed
  • Each re-telling strengthens the groove. Accept, don't excavate

Evidence

This is the weakest-evidenced practice here, and it should be labelled honestly. There is no substantial peer-reviewed literature on "retroactive jealousy" as such — the term is largely clinical and popular. What is well established is adjacent: rumination maintains and amplifies distress rather than resolving it, and acceptance-based approaches reduce it more reliably than analysis does. Nolen-Hoeksema et al. (2008); Hayes et al. (2006)4,5
Extrapolated from adjacent research
05

Our pace is set together

Neither pressure nor retreat.

In practice

  • Learn which one you are under stress — the pursuer or the withdrawer
  • Name the cycle, not the person: "we're doing the thing again"
  • The pursuer softens the demand; the withdrawer stays in the room. Both, or neither works

Evidence

Emotionally Focused Therapy treats the pursue–withdraw cycle as the engine of distress rather than a symptom of it. Across randomised trials, roughly 70–73% of couples moved from distress to recovery; a 2022 meta-analysis across 20 studies found a large pre–post effect (d = 0.93) that held at follow-up. Johnson et al. (1999); Spengler et al. (2022)6,7
Strong · see sampling limits
06

One protected evening a week

Phones away, fully present.

In practice

  • Protected means it survives a busy week — otherwise it isn't protected
  • Phones in another room, not face-down on the table. Presence is measurable
  • Responsiveness is the point: understood, validated, cared for. Not merely co-located

Evidence

Reis and Shaver's interpersonal process model holds that intimacy is built through disclosure met with a response the discloser reads as understanding, validating and caring — perceived partner responsiveness. McDaniel and Coyne's work on "technoference" found everyday device interruptions predicted lower relationship satisfaction, mediated by conflict over technology use. Reis & Shaver (1988); McDaniel & Coyne (2016)8,9
Model well supported · technoference correlational
07

Safety first in intimacy

Unhurried, private, listening to each other's bodies.

In practice

  • Stop waiting for spontaneous desire to arrive and then reading its absence as a verdict
  • Context is not a mood-killer; context is the mechanism
  • Low pressure is not the preliminary to desire — it's the precondition

Evidence

Basson's circular model reframed desire as frequently responsive — emerging after arousal begins, in conditions of safety — rather than spontaneous and preceding it. Nagoski's dual control model adds that reducing the "brakes" (stress, pressure, self-consciousness) usually matters more than pressing the accelerator. Basson (2000); Nagoski (2015)10,11
Influential model · clinically derived
08

Celebrate each other's wins

We show up loudly for good news, not just faithfully for bad.

In practice

  • Four ways to answer good news; only one builds anything — active and constructive
  • "That's great" while scrolling is passive-constructive. It reads as indifference
  • Ask for the details. Make them tell it twice

Evidence

Gable's work on capitalization found that how a partner responds to good news predicts relationship well-being, commitment and longevity — in several analyses more strongly than support offered during adversity. Enthusiastic, engaged responses signal investment; passive ones quietly corrode. Gable, Reis, Impett & Asher (2004); Gable et al. (2006)12,13
Well replicated
09

Say thank you, specifically, weekly

Gratitude unspoken doesn't count.

In practice

  • Name the act, not the trait: "you took the early train so I could sleep"
  • Felt gratitude has no measurable effect on your partner. Expressed gratitude does
  • Weekly is the floor, not the ceiling

Evidence

Algoe's find–remind–bind theory holds that expressed gratitude functions as a relationship booster: in daily-diary work, a partner's expression of gratitude predicted both partners' relationship connection and satisfaction the following day. The expression is the active ingredient. Algoe, Gable & Maisel (2010); Algoe (2012)14,15
Well supported
10

Check in on us quarterly

Review, adjust, keep building.

In practice

  • Put it in the calendar. A relationship review that depends on remembering won't happen
  • Three questions: what's working, what's thinning, what do we change before next quarter
  • Treat it as maintenance, not as an alarm. You review when things are fine

Evidence

Finkel's "all-or-nothing" account argues modern marriages ask more of a partner than ever and deliver more when adequately invested in — but that investment has to be deliberate. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, running since 1938 across 724 original participants, found relationship satisfaction at 50 predicted physical health at 80 better than cholesterol did. Finkel (2017); Waldinger & Schulz (2023)16,17
Landmark · observational, not causal

Limitations

Read this

Most of this is correlational. The Harvard Study is observational — it establishes that warm relationships travel with better health across decades, not that one causes the other. Couples who repair well may simply be couples who were doing better already. Only the therapy trials (practice 5) are randomised.

Gottman's prediction rates are disputed. The widely cited "90%+ accuracy" figures came from models fitted to the same samples they predicted — retrodiction, not prediction. Heyman and Slep's re-analysis showed accuracy drops substantially on independent data. The behaviours Gottman identifies remain well supported; the headline accuracy claim does not.

The samples skew narrow. EFT's efficacy research has been conducted largely with White, middle-class, heterosexual couples, and cannot be assumed to transfer without adaptation. This is true of much of the field: predominantly Western, educated, industrialised, rich and democratic samples.

Effect sizes are not destiny. A large average effect says nothing certain about any individual couple. These are practices with reasonable evidentiary support — not laws, and not a guarantee.
